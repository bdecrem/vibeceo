# Free Agent CRM — v1 Scope

## The User

Consultants, freelancers, solo founders, investors, community builders, creators. People whose network *is* their career infrastructure. They don't think in pipelines or deal stages — they think in relationships, contexts, and "I should really get back to that person."

They currently track people in a mess of spreadsheets, Apple Notes, memory, and guilt.

## The Job to Be Done

**"Help me be the person who stays in touch."**

Not "help me close deals." Not "help me manage customers." Help me remember who people are, how I know them, and when it's time to reach out — so that no important relationship goes cold by accident.

---

## V1 Feature Set

### 1. Add a Person (the core object)

**Fields:** Name (required), how we met / context note (freeform, optional but prompted), notes (freeform scratchpad), last contacted (date, manually set or auto-updated when logging an interaction), tags (freeform, user-created, multi-select).

**What's intentionally missing from v1:** photo, phone, email, social links. These feel essential but they're *address book fields*, and the phone already has an address book. This isn't a contact manager — it's a relationship manager. If users scream for contact fields, add them in v2. Don't let the product become a worse version of Apple Contacts.

**Design note:** The "how we met" field is the secret weapon. Six months later, the difference between a useful entry and a useless one is whether you wrote "Met at Dan's dinner party, works on climate policy, funny, knows Sarah" or just "Maria Chen."

### 2. Log an Interaction

**Fields:** Date (defaults to today), type (lightweight — coffee, call, text, email, event, other), note (freeform — what you talked about, what you promised, what you learned), people involved (can tag multiple people — group dinners are real).

**Behavior:** Logging an interaction auto-updates "last contacted" for all linked people. Interactions show up on a person's detail view as a timeline. They also live in a global feed (reverse chron) so you can browse "what have I been up to."

**What's intentionally missing:** Automatic logging (email sync, calendar sync). V2/v3 feature, massive scope increase. V1 is manual and that's fine — the act of writing a note is itself valuable. It forces reflection.

### 3. Tags

Freeform text, user-created, many-to-many with people. Created inline when adding/editing a person (type to create or select existing). Filterable — "show me everyone tagged #angel-investor." No hierarchy, no categories, no tag management UI. Just a flat namespace.

**How free agents actually use tags:** by relationship type (`#mentor`, `#collaborator`, `#client`), by context (`#yc-batch`, `#climate-tech`, `#portland`), by action (`#warm-intro-possible`, `#potential-client`), by vibe (`#creative`, `#deep-thinker`, `#connector`).

**Design note:** Tags and "how we met" together do the work that a rigid company/role/industry schema would do in a traditional CRM — but they're flexible enough for the way free agents actually categorize people (which is idiosyncratic and contextual).

### 4. The Reconnect Queue

This is the feature that makes it a product and not a spreadsheet.

**How it works:** Every person has a "last contacted" date. The user sets a **desired contact frequency** per person: weekly, monthly, quarterly, yearly, or "no reminder." The app computes who is "overdue" — last contacted + desired frequency < today. The main screen surfaces a prioritized list: "People to reconnect with." Sorted by how overdue they are, most overdue first. That's it for v1 — no ML, no relationship strength score, no complexity.

**Actions from the queue:**
- **Log interaction** (I reached out, here's what happened) → clears them from the queue
- **Snooze** (not now, remind me in 1 week / 1 month) → temporarily removes from queue
- **Skip** (I've decided I don't need to reach out) → resets the timer without logging an interaction. Honest and non-judgmental.

**Design note:** The queue should feel like a gentle nudge, not a guilt machine. Tone matters enormously. "You haven't talked to Maria in 3 months" is accusatory. "Maria — it's been a while. Coffee?" is warm. The UX copy carries real weight here.

### 5. Search and Browse

Search by name, tag, or freeform text across notes and interaction notes. Browse by tag (filter), by recency (last contacted), alphabetical. Person detail view shows name, tags, how-we-met note, freeform notes, and interaction timeline.

That's it. No dashboards, no analytics, no visualizations. V1 is about *using* the tool daily, not admiring your network.

## Data Model

```
Person
├── id
├── name
├── how_we_met (text, nullable)
├── notes (text, nullable)
├── last_contacted (date, nullable)
├── desired_frequency (enum: weekly | monthly | quarterly | yearly | none)
├── created_at
└── updated_at

Tag
├── id
├── label (unique, user-created)
└── created_at

PersonTag (join table)
├── person_id → Person
└── tag_id → Tag

Interaction
├── id
├── date
├── type (enum: coffee | call | text | email | event | other)
├── note (text, nullable)
└── created_at

InteractionPerson (join table)
├── interaction_id → Interaction
└── person_id → Person
```

**Key modeling decisions:**

**Interactions link to multiple people.** A dinner with three people is one interaction, not three. When you look at Maria's timeline and see "Dinner with Maria, James, and Aisha — talked about the nonprofit launch," that's *way* more useful than three separate entries.

**No "Company" or "Context" entity in v1.** Use tags instead. `#stripe`, `#yc-w24`, `#climate-council` do the job without introducing a second entity type with its own CRUD, its own relationship to people, and its own UI. If users start wanting "show me everyone at Stripe" as a first-class feature, that's the signal to promote contexts to a real entity in v2.

**Desired frequency lives on Person, not on a separate Reminder entity.** V1 doesn't need arbitrary reminders ("remind me to email James on March 15"). It needs recurring rhythms. Every person has a cadence, and the system tells you when you're behind. Arbitrary reminders are v2.

**Last contacted is denormalized.** It could be computed from MAX(interaction.date), but storing it directly on Person means (a) you can set it without logging a full interaction, and (b) the reconnect queue query is trivial.

## What's Explicitly Out of Scope for V1

| Feature | Why it's out | When it comes in |
|---|---|---|
| Contact info (phone, email, social) | Address book feature. The phone already does this. | V2, if users ask |
| Company/org as first-class entity | Tags handle it. Orgs mean CRUD, linking UI, roles — too much surface area. | V2 |
| Automatic interaction logging | Massive integration scope. OAuth, parsing, deduplication, privacy. | V3 at earliest |
| Relationship strength scores | Needs data and a scoring model. Premature. | V2 |
| Birthday tracking | Nice-to-have, not core to the JTBD. | V2 |
| Import/export | Important for onboarding but not for validating the concept. | V2 |
| Sharing / multiplayer | This is a personal tool. | Probably never |
| Native mobile app | Responsive web app first. | V2 if concept validates |
| AI features | Exciting but premature. Get the manual version right. | V2/V3 |

## The V1 Daily Loop

If the daily loop doesn't feel natural, nothing else matters.

**Morning (2 minutes):** Open the app. The reconnect queue is front and center. See 3 people to reconnect with. Decide: text Maria today, snooze James to next week, skip Aisha.

**After a coffee/call (1 minute):** Log interaction. Select Maria, type: "Coffee at Sightglass. She's leaving Stripe, exploring climate startups. Offered to intro her to David." Maria's last-contacted updates, she drops off the queue.

**Meet someone new (1 minute):** Add person: "Raj Patel." How we met: "SaaStr conference, runs a DevTools startup, knows Emily from college." Tags: `#founder`, `#devtools`, `#sastr-2025`. Set frequency: quarterly.

**Weekly review (5 minutes, optional):** Browse the reconnect queue. Anyone you've been snoozing repeatedly? Either reach out or change their frequency to "yearly" or "none." Be honest with yourself.

## Success Criteria

The product is working if: a user adds 20+ people in their first week, returns 3x/week to check the reconnect queue, logs 2+ interactions per week, and after 30 days would be annoyed if you took it away.

The product is *not* working if: people add contacts but never come back (it's a dump, not a tool), the reconnect queue feels like a chore, or users immediately ask "but where do I put their email?" (you've built a worse address book, not a better relationship tool).

## Open Questions Before Building

1. **Web or native?** Recommendation: responsive web app (PWA-capable). Validates faster, works everywhere, avoids app store friction.

2. **Auth & storage?** Simple email/password auth, Postgres, REST API. The data is low-volume and highly structured. Don't overthink it.

3. **Empty state problem.** The app is useless with zero people. Consider a guided "add 5 people you've been meaning to reach out to" onboarding flow.

4. **Tone of voice.** The reconnect queue copy matters enormously. Workshop it. The difference between "overdue" and "it's been a while" is the difference between guilt-ware and a warm nudge.

5. **Single-player forever?** If always personal, architectural decisions get simpler — no permissions, no sharing, no team features. Worth deciding explicitly now.
