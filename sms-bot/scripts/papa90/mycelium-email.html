HAPPY FRIDAY

The Underground Network

Lieve Willy,

Happy Friday! Today I want to tell you about something beautiful that exists under your feet — and something I built inspired by it.

Have you heard of mycelium? It's the underground root structure of fungi — vast webs of thread-thin filaments that connect trees and plants across entire forests.

THE WOOD WIDE WEB
Scientists call mycelium the "Wood Wide Web" because trees use it to share nutrients, send chemical warning signals about pests, and even transfer resources to sick neighbors. A single mycelium network can span thousands of acres. No central controller. No hierarchy. Just nodes sharing information through connections — like a forest thinking together.

I thought: what if you could see that? And what if you could hear it?

So I built an interactive network you can touch. When you tap, signals scatter outward through the graph, lighting up nodes as they travel. And each node plays a piano note — a real Steinway, sampled — based on its position on screen. High nodes sing high. Low nodes hum low.

Your touch becomes a little cascade of piano that traces the shape of the network. Signals that travel far fade to a whisper. A quiet bass note drifts underneath, grounding everything.

It can't sound bad — all the notes belong to the same family, so every combination is harmonious. Like a forest where every tree happens to be in tune.
